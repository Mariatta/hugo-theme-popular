Welcome & how to use this

Thank you for helping run Your Community. Organizing is a team effort, and you are never expected to do it alone. This handbook is the why and what, the principles and expectations. The Runbooks are the how, tick-box checklists you follow the week of an event.

Our north star

Every decision comes back to one question: will this help someone who has never been to a meetup feel welcome here? If yes, we’re on the right track.

Keep this handbook open in a browser tab during planning. Share it freely with new volunteers, nothing here is secret.

Roles & responsibilities

We keep roles lightweight. One person can wear several hats, and hats rotate so no one burns out.

  • Lead organizer: owns the calendar, confirms each event, and is the final point of contact.
  • Venue coordinator: sources and confirms the space, checks accessibility, and is the on-site contact for the host.
  • Session wrangler: finds and briefs session hosts, collects intros and titles, and looks after them on the night.
  • Comms lead: creates the event page, writes announcements, and posts recaps across our channels.
  • Welcome host: the friendly face at the door: greets arrivals and makes sure no newcomer stands alone.
Two-deep rule

Aim for at least two organizers at every event. If one person is unwell or stuck in traffic, the meetup still happens.

Meeting cadence & format

We aim for one gathering a month, usually a weekday evening from 6:00 to 8:30 PM. Formats rotate to keep things fresh and to serve everyone:

  • Session night: one or two hosts lead a topic, with time for questions. The easiest format to run.
  • Hands-on night: everyone participates and makes something. Needs helpers roaming the room.
  • Show & tell: five-minute shares, great for first-timers.
  • Social: a lower-effort month to just connect the community.

A dependable rhythm matters more than a perfect one. If a month is quiet, run a small social rather than skipping entirely.

Choosing a venue

A good venue is the single biggest lever on how welcoming an event feels. Look for:

  • Capacity for 20–40 people, seated, with room to mingle.
  • Step-free access and an accessible washroom, this is non-negotiable.
  • A screen or space suited to your format, and any equipment your sessions need.
  • Within a short walk of transit or with easy parking.
  • A host contact who can let us in, and stay or delegate a key holder.
Always visit first

Confirm the room, the setup and the accessible entrance in person (or via a trusted host) before you announce the event. Surprises on the night are stressful for everyone.

Finding session hosts

Great hosts are all around us, including in our own audience. We actively lower the bar to leading a session:

  • Invite first-timers and offer to co-host or rehearse with them.
  • Keep an ongoing list of members who’ve mentioned a topic they love.
  • Ask past attendees what they’d like to learn, then find someone to lead it.
  • Reach out to neighbouring community groups for cross-promotion.
  • Always confirm a title, a 2–3 sentence intro, and a photo for promotion.

What we tell every new host: no experience necessary. Whether it’s your first time leading a session or your fiftieth, we welcome you, and we’ll help you prepare.

Promotion & communications

Consistency beats cleverness. Post early, post again, and always include the essentials: what, when, where, and how to RSVP.

  • Create the event on your RSVP platform as the single source of truth.
  • Announce ~2 weeks out, remind ~3 days out, and post a “tonight!” nudge on the day.
  • Cross-post to your usual channels.
  • Use a consistent poster template so every announcement looks unmistakably yours.
  • Write a short recap within a week, photos (with consent) and a thank-you to hosts.

Budget & sponsorship

Our events are free to attend and run on a shoestring. Most costs are covered in-kind by venue hosts.

  • Typical costs: light snacks and drinks. Venues and equipment are usually donated.
  • Ask sponsors for in-kind support (space, food, supplies) before cash.
  • Thank every sponsor publicly, by name, with a link.
  • Keep a simple shared spreadsheet of what was spent and who paid.
  • Never require payment or personal financial details from attendees.

Safety & Code of Conduct

Every event runs under our Code of Conduct. As an organizer you are also a first responder for it.

  • Open every event with a brief, warm reminder of the Code of Conduct.
  • Make sure attendees know which organizers to approach with a concern.
  • Take every report seriously, listen without judgement, and protect the reporter’s privacy.
  • You can ask anyone to leave whose behaviour makes others unsafe, you have that authority.
  • Debrief with a co-organizer after any incident and log what happened privately.
When in doubt, prioritize safety

It is always better to pause an activity, remove a disruptive person, or end early than to let a harmful situation continue. The community will back you.

Day-of logistics

The Day-of runbook has the full checklist. In short:

  • Arrive 45–60 minutes early to set up the space and the welcome table.
  • Put up simple signage from the entrance to the room.
  • Greet every person as they arrive; hand newcomers a friendly hello, not a form.
  • Start within 10 minutes of the hour; keep sessions to time so people catch transit home.
  • Take a few photos for the recap, always ask before photographing anyone.

After the event

The event isn’t over when the room empties. A tidy wrap-up keeps hosts happy and the community informed.

  • Leave the venue cleaner than you found it and thank the host in person.
  • Send thank-you notes to hosts and sponsors within 48 hours.
  • Publish a recap post with photos and shout-outs within a week.
  • Note what worked and what didn’t in the shared organizer log.
  • Lock in a date for next month before momentum fades.
Better late than never

If a recap slips, publish it anyway. Our community would rather read a late recap than none at all.
